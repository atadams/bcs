Deciding When To Start Breast Cancer Screening: A Decision Aid for Average-Risk Women Aged 40–49

This decision aid is for women who are in their 40s, are at average risk for breast cancer, and do not have any signs or symptoms of breast cancer. If you have a change in your breast that worries you, talk with your health care professional.

The purpose of this decision aid is to help you learn about the potential benefits, and harms of breast cancer screening and help you decide if starting regular screening in your 40s is the right choice for you. This is not a one-time decision. You and your health care professional may talk about whether to start screening more than once.

Screening is a personal decision a woman makes with her health care professional. Some women may decide to start screening in their 40s instead of in their 50s because they feel the potential benefits of screening in their 40s is greater than the potential harms. Other women may decide to wait until their 50s because they feel the potential harms of screening in their 40s are greater than the potential benefits.

This decision aid does not include information about breast cancer treatments.

Who is average risk?

Women who are at average risk for breast cancer do not have signs or symptoms of breast cancer, have never been diagnosed with breast cancer, and are not at high risk for breast cancer.

To find out if you are at average risk for breast cancer, complete “Is this decision aid right for me?”

What is breast cancer?

Breast cancer is the second most commonly diagnosed cancer for women in the United States behind skin cancer. Breast cancer happens when there are cells in the breast that are not normal. These cells do not grow like healthy cells. They can continue to grow and spread to other parts of the body.

What is breast cancer screening?

The goal of breast cancer screening is to find breast cancer early before signs or symptoms appear. When breast cancer is found at an early stage, it may be easier to treat or cure. But, there are also some potential harms with breast cancer screening. To learn more about the potential benefits and harms of breast cancer screening, see What are the potential benefits and harms of breast cancer screening for average-risk women aged 40–49 years?

What test is used for breast cancer screening?

Mammograms are the most common screening test for breast cancer

If you get a mammogram, your breasts will be put between two plastic plates in a machine that will take a picture with x-rays.

One breast at a time is put between the plastic plates. To get a clear picture, the plates will be pressed together for less than a minute to flatten your breasts. Some women may find this uncomfortable.

The mammogram machine will take a picture of the inside of each breast. Then an expert will look at the pictures. If the pictures find something that may be cancer, then you may need more testing to see if it is cancer. Breast cancer can only be diagnosed with a biopsy.

What about clinical breast exams and self-breast exams?

Clinical breast exams and self-breast exams are not recommended for breast cancer screening. If you notice any changes in your breast, you should talk with your health care professional.

What is the recommendation for breast cancer screening for average-risk women aged 40–49 years?

The U.S. Preventive Services Task Force (USPSTF) recommends that women who are 40 to 49 years old and are at average risk for breast cancer make an individual choice with their health care professional about when to start breast cancer screening.

The USPSTF is a group of volunteer scientific experts in preventive medicine that review the latest research findings to make recommendations for clinical preventive care.

The USPSTF recommends that women get screened for breast cancer every other year.

Some women may decide to start breast cancer screening in their 40s instead of in their 50s because they feel the potential benefits of screening is greater than the potential harms. Other women may decide to wait until their 50s because they feel the potential harms of screening is greater than the potential benefits.

Talk with your health care professional to decide what is best for you.

Why did the USPSTF make this recommendation?

Researchers found that breast cancer screening lowers the chance of dying from breast cancer. However, breast cancer is less common in women in their 40s. Also, younger women are more likely than older women to have dense breasts. Dense breasts makes it harder to find cancer

Researchers found that the potential benefits of screening were about the same if women get screened every year or every other year. But with more frequent screening, women are exposed to more potential harms.

Harm: Radiation exposure

Radiation exposure can increase the risk of a second cancer. Breast cancer screening exposes women to 0.4 millisieverts of radiation. We do not know how much radiation is too much, especially when women are exposed to small amounts of radiation over many years from breast cancer screening.

Deciding With Your Health Care Professional

Making the decision about when to start breast cancer screening is a personal decision. Talk with your health care professional about what is best for you.

Below are some questions to help you talk with your health care professional.

  • How can breast cancer screening help me?
  • How can breast cancer screening harm me?

Additional questions you may want to ask your health care professional:

  • Do I have to do anything to get ready for screening?
  • How soon will I know the results, and who will explain them to me?
  • What do I do if my results suggest that I may have cancer?
  • Does my insurance cover the cost of breast cancer screening? What about followup testing?
  • What can I do to lower my risk of breast cancer?
